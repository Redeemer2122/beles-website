# BELES Engineering Playbook v1.0 — Strategy & Experience

> Project: BELES Group website  
> Purpose: Define what the website must communicate, how the user experience should flow, and how each page supports the business story.  
> Use this document as the main source of truth before building pages, sections, content, and interactions.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

BELES should not be presented as a regular vehicle manufacturer or a standard corporate company.  
The website must position BELES as an **engineering-led automotive and mobility company** with national significance, industrial ambition, and practical manufacturing capability.

The site should feel like a premium digital experience, but not like a luxury fashion brand. The visual and narrative language should communicate:

- industrial confidence;
- engineering discipline;
- national progress;
- modern mobility;
- production capability;
- long-term trust.

The main goal is simple:

> A visitor should quickly understand that BELES is building something serious, technical, and future-facing.

This does not require heavy decoration, excessive animations, or complicated UI. It requires strong composition, clear narrative, controlled motion, and proof-based storytelling.

## 2. Product Positioning

### 2.1 What BELES is

BELES is positioned as:

- an engineering and manufacturing company;
- a national automotive / mobility platform;
- a company capable of developing commercial vehicles and mobility solutions;
- a bridge between industrial production, technology partnerships, and regional market needs.

### 2.2 What BELES is not

BELES should not be framed as:

- a simple bus seller;
- a generic factory website;
- a slideshow of certificates;
- a PDF converted into web pages;
- a decorative “premium” website without substance.

### 2.3 Core positioning sentence

> BELES develops and manufactures mobility solutions for a new industrial era in Central Asia.

This sentence is not necessarily the final homepage headline. It is the positioning logic behind the whole project.

## 3. Experience Principles

These principles should guide every design, content, and implementation decision.

### 3.1 Engineering before marketing

Do not rely on empty claims like “innovative”, “modern”, or “high quality” unless the page shows why.

Better pattern:

1. Show the machine, process, detail, production, certification, or technical system.
2. Then explain the value.
3. Then invite the user to explore deeper.

### 3.2 Premium through precision

Premium does not mean gold gradients or decorative effects.  
For BELES, premium means:

- disciplined spacing;
- strong typography;
- clear hierarchy;
- confident silence;
- accurate alignment;
- high contrast;
- restrained interaction;
- purposeful motion.

### 3.3 Story before specification

Specifications matter, but the first impression should not feel like a technical datasheet.  
The user should first understand the mission, then the capability, then the details.

### 3.4 Scenes, not sections

The homepage should be designed as a sequence of scenes.

A normal section says:

> Here is information.

A scene says:

> Here is a moment in the story.

Each scene must have:

- one main idea;
- one visual focus;
- one emotional intention;
- one next step.

### 3.5 Motion with purpose

Motion must never exist only because it looks expensive.  
Every animation must serve at least one of these purposes:

- reveal;
- transition;
- focus;
- explanation;
- orientation;
- rhythm.

### 3.6 Mobile is not a reduced desktop

The mobile version should not be treated as a broken-down desktop layout.  
Mobile should have its own rhythm: shorter scenes, faster reveals, simpler motion, stronger text hierarchy.

## 5. Page Roles

### 5.1 Home

Role: create the first emotional and strategic impression.

The homepage should answer:

- Who is BELES?
- Why does it matter?
- What does it build?
- Can this company be trusted?
- Where should I go next?

The homepage should not explain everything. It should create momentum.

### 5.2 Story

Role: explain the origin, mission, national significance, and long-term direction.

The Story page should feel human and strategic, not like a corporate chronology.

It should answer:

- Why was BELES created?
- What problem does it solve?
- Why is it important for the region?
- What future does it point toward?

### 5.3 Projects

Role: prove that BELES is real.

Projects should be the strongest evidence layer of the site.

This page should include:

- flagship vehicles;
- engineering projects;
- production programs;
- future concepts if clearly labeled;
- case-study style content.

### 5.4 Capabilities

Role: explain what BELES can do.

This is the page for:

- engineering;
- production;
- assembly;
- certification;
- partner integration;
- EV/CNG direction;
- scalable manufacturing logic.

### 5.5 About

Role: humanize the company.

This page should show:

- leadership;
- team;
- values;
- culture;
- history;
- credibility markers.

It should not duplicate the Story page.

### 5.6 Contact

Role: reduce friction.

The Contact page should be simple, fast, and clear.  
No over-designed forms. No unnecessary steps.

## 6. Homepage Experience Script

The homepage should be built as a controlled narrative.  
It should not feel like a random collection of blocks.

### Scene 01 — Arrival

**Goal:** Stop the user immediately.  
**Emotion:** Curiosity, confidence, seriousness.  
**Message:** BELES is not just a manufacturer; it is an engineering force.  
**Visual direction:** Large typography, restrained interface, one strong industrial or vehicle-related image.  
**Motion:** Slow reveal, not theatrical delay. User should not feel blocked.

Content pattern:

```text
Engineering mobility for a new industrial era.
```

Alternative directions:

```text
Built in Kyrgyzstan. Engineered for the road ahead.
```

```text
Industrial mobility, designed for real cities.
```

Avoid:

```text
Welcome to BELES Group
```

```text
We are a modern innovative company
```

### Scene 02 — National / Regional Context

**Goal:** Explain why BELES matters.  
**Emotion:** Scale, ambition.  
**Message:** BELES is part of a larger industrial shift.  
**Visual direction:** Map-like abstractions, production silhouettes, geographic references, transport routes.  
**Motion:** Subtle spatial movement; not a literal animated map unless well designed.

Key idea:

> BELES exists at the intersection of engineering, production, and regional mobility demand.

### Scene 03 — Engineering Process

**Goal:** Shift from brand claim to proof.  
**Emotion:** Trust.  
**Message:** The company has real process, not just presentation.  
**Visual direction:** Technical drawings, assembly details, production imagery, component close-ups.  
**Motion:** Layered reveals, blueprint-like sequencing, controlled parallax.

Key pattern:

- show process first;
- then show product;
- then show result.

### Scene 04 — Vehicle / Project Reveal

**Goal:** Present the product as the outcome of engineering.  
**Emotion:** Recognition, clarity.  
**Message:** BELES builds real commercial mobility solutions.  
**Visual direction:** Hero vehicle image, side profile, front detail, interior/cabin details.  
**Motion:** Strong but clean reveal; avoid spinning 3D gimmicks unless assets are excellent.

### Scene 05 — Capabilities Grid

**Goal:** Explain breadth without overwhelming.  
**Emotion:** Confidence.  
**Message:** BELES can operate across engineering, production, certification, and delivery.  
**Visual direction:** Editorial grid, large labels, compact explanations.  
**Motion:** Minimal stagger reveals.

Suggested capability groups:

- Vehicle engineering
- Production and assembly
- Certification and compliance
- Technology integration
- Commercial transport solutions
- Future mobility systems

### Scene 06 — Projects Preview

**Goal:** Invite deeper exploration.  
**Emotion:** Momentum.  
**Message:** The company has tangible projects worth exploring.  
**Visual direction:** Three to five project cards with strong imagery and short descriptions.  
**Motion:** Horizontal or vertical editorial reveal, not a carousel-first design.

### Scene 07 — Future Direction

**Goal:** Make the brand feel forward-looking.  
**Emotion:** Aspiration.  
**Message:** BELES is building a platform for future mobility, not only current vehicles.  
**Visual direction:** Abstract industrial future, EV/CNG, digital systems, city mobility.  
**Motion:** Calm, atmospheric, not sci-fi cliché.

### Scene 08 — Final CTA

**Goal:** Convert interest into action.  
**Emotion:** Clarity.  
**Message:** Contact BELES, explore projects, or learn about capabilities.  
**Visual direction:** Simple layout, strong CTA, no clutter.  
**Motion:** Minimal.

## 7. Page Blueprints

## 7.1 Story Page Blueprint

### Purpose

Explain the deeper meaning behind BELES.

### Recommended structure

1. Opening statement
2. Origin and context
3. Industrial ambition
4. Engineering mission
5. Regional relevance
6. Future vision
7. CTA to Projects or Capabilities

### Tone

Confident, serious, human.

- long generic company history;
- timeline overload;
- excessive patriotic language without engineering substance.

Show proof.

### Recommended structure

1. Page hero
2. Featured project
3. Project index
4. Project detail cards
5. Engineering facts
6. Media gallery
7. CTA to contact / capabilities

### Project card should include

- project name;
- category;
- one-line role;
- short description;
- key facts;

- Commercial vehicles
- Public transport
- Electric / CNG direction
- Engineering programs
- Future mobility concepts

## 7.5 Contact Page Blueprint

### Purpose

Make it easy to reach the company.

### Recommended structure

1. Short headline
2. Contact options
3. Business inquiry form
4. Location / map if appropriate
5. Direct contact details
6. Partnership CTA

### Form fields

Keep the form small:

- name;
- company;
- email / phone;
- inquiry type;
- message.

## 8. Content Strategy

### 8.1 Messaging hierarchy

The website should speak in this order:

1. Big mission
2. Engineering capability
3. Product proof
4. Technical detail
5. Contact / partnership

### 8.2 Writing style

Use:

- short sentences;
- confident verbs;
- concrete nouns;
- engineering language;
- active voice.

Avoid:

- generic marketing adjectives;
- vague innovation language;
- long paragraphs;
- corporate clichés.

### 8.3 Good sentence patterns

```text
Designed for public transport systems that need reliability at scale.
```

```text
Built around engineering, certification, and production discipline.
```

```text
From concept to assembly, BELES develops mobility solutions for real operating conditions.
```

### 8.4 Bad sentence patterns

```text
We are a dynamically developing innovative company.
```

```text
Our team provides high-quality solutions using modern technologies.
```

```text
We always strive to satisfy customer needs.
```

## 9. User Journeys

### 9.1 Potential partner

Needs to understand:

- whether BELES is credible;
- what capabilities exist;
- how to contact the company.

Recommended journey:

Home → Capabilities → Projects → Contact

### 9.2 Government / institutional visitor

Needs to understand:

- national significance;
- public transport relevance;
- certification and production seriousness.

Recommended journey:

Home → Story → Capabilities → Projects → Contact

### 9.3 Investor / strategic stakeholder

Needs to understand:

- scale;
- future direction;
- proof;
- team.

Recommended journey:

Home → Story → Projects → About → Contact

### 9.4 General visitor

Needs to understand:

- what BELES is;
- why the site feels impressive;
- what the company makes.

Recommended journey:

Home → Projects → Story

---

## 10. Success Criteria

The website is successful if:

- the first screen feels strong and memorable;
- visitors understand BELES within 10 seconds;
- the homepage feels like a story, not a template;
- projects feel real and credible;
- capabilities are clear without being boring;
- mobile experience feels intentional;
- performance remains high;
- future content can be added without redesigning the system.

---

## 11. Implementation Priority

Recommended order:

1. Global layout and navigation
2. Design tokens
3. Homepage scenes
4. Shared section primitives
5. Projects data model
6. Capabilities content structure
7. SEO and metadata system
8. Motion layer
9. Page templates
10. CMS preparation

Do not start with visual decoration.  
Start with structure, then narrative, then motion, then polish.
